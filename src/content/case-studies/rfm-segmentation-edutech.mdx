## Problem

The EduTech growth team had a budget for re-engagement and no principled way to
spend it. Outreach was effectively "email everyone" or "email whoever a sales
rep remembered". Some of the people being chased had bought last week; some had
been gone for a year and were never coming back. Effort was uniform across a
population that was anything but.

The question underneath was concrete: of all our learners, which few hundred
should a human actually call this week to move revenue — and which should we
leave alone?

## Approach

RFM — Recency, Frequency, Monetary — is the standard lens for exactly this, so
I started there rather than inventing something clever. Each learner gets scored
on how recently they purchased, how often, and how much. The scores bucket into
segments with obvious operational meaning: Champions (recent, frequent, high
value), At-Risk (used to be valuable, going quiet), and Hibernating (long gone,
low value).

RFM tells you the *state*; it doesn't tell you the *play*. To get from segment
to action I paired it with Amplitude's *Mastering Retention* playbook, which
frames retention as distinct motions for distinct cohorts — protect the
Champions, intervene on the At-Risk before they're gone, and don't overspend
reactivating the Hibernating. The combination turned a scatter of scores into a
short list of named groups with a recommended motion attached to each.

## Architecture

This rode on the same platform as the ops work rather than a separate stack:

- Customer transaction history extracted via the existing **Prefect** flows and
  landed in **MinIO**.
- **DuckDB** computed the RFM scores — recency from last purchase date,
  frequency as a purchase count over the window, monetary as summed value —
  then bucketed each dimension into quantiles and mapped the combinations to
  named segments.
- **Metabase** rendered the segments as an editorial scatter (recency against
  frequency, point size by monetary value) plus a plain table the growth team
  could filter and, critically, export as a call list.

Keeping it on the shared platform meant the segmentation refreshed on the same
schedule as everything else, instead of being a notebook someone ran by hand
once and never again.

## Outcome

The growth team stopped treating the base as one undifferentiated list. Three
cohorts came out with clear motions: Champions to protect and upsell, At-Risk to
intervene on while intervention still works, and Hibernating to deprioritise.
The tangible artefact was a ranked, exportable list of who to contact — finite,
prioritised, and refreshed automatically rather than reconstructed from memory.

The framing mattered as much as the math. "Call these 200 At-Risk learners
before they churn" is a sentence a sales lead can act on this morning; "here is
the RFM matrix" is not.

## Reflection

RFM is descriptive, not predictive — it tells you where someone is, not where
they're heading. The natural next step is a propensity model that estimates
churn probability and expected value so the call list ranks by likely impact,
not just current state.

I'd also instrument the loop end to end: tag who got contacted, from which
cohort, with which motion, and watch their next-period RFM movement. Without
that feedback the segmentation is a reasonable hypothesis; with it, it becomes
a system that learns which interventions actually move people between cohorts.
